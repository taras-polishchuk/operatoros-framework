## TL;DR — The single sentence

The smallest, most defensible set of canonical questions a senior engineer asks about a workspace is **eleven questions** in **six groups**, of which OperatorOS's current proposed ecosystem answers **eight well, two adequately, and one not at all** — the unanswered question is the strongest finding here.

The eleven questions, in frequency order:

1. **What is this?** *(identity)*
2. **Where do I start?** *(entry point)*
3. **What is the current architecture?** *(structure)*
4. **What changed recently?** *(history)*
5. **What is missing?** *(gaps)*
6. **What is risky?** *(risk)*
7. **What will confuse an AI?** *(agent-readiness)*
8. **What decisions shaped this?** *(intent / provenance)*
9. **Who am I in this workspace?** *(operator identity)*
10. **What is the work I'm doing here?** *(mission / task context)*
11. **How do I extend this?** *(extension contract)*

The grouping that the eleven fold into, six groups of *kinds-of-question*:

- **Identify** (Q1) — what *is* this?
- **Orient** (Q2, Q11) — where do I start, and how do I grow it?
- **Map** (Q3, Q7) — what's the structure, and what does an agent see in it?
- **Audit** (Q4, Q5, Q6) — what changed, what is missing, what is risky?
- **Contextualize** (Q8, Q9, Q10) — why, who, what for?

The single currently-unanswered question is **Q9, "Who am I in this workspace?"** An OperatorOS workspace currently has `bootstrap.md` (read by the AI), `operatoros.yaml` (the manifest), and `state/` (runtime data) — but no first-class canonical place for *the engineer's own identity inside that workspace*. This is the gap most likely to demand a new module in v0.9.0 or v0.10.0. A telling detail: `identity-md` exists in the v0.8.0 ships-set, but it answers *Q10* (the work the engineer does in this workspace), not Q9 (who the engineer *is*).

Three proposed modules are also flagged as **redundant**: `catalog-validator` (Quality), `schema-bridge` (Quality), and `preset-applier` (Workspace) are each answerable by `drift-detector` + the catalog itself, or by `operatoros validate <file>`, or by `operatoros apply`. The marginal value of each is small; the maintenance cost is recurring.

Six modules are flagged as **missing**: `inspect` (Core capability), `context-builder`, `architecture-index`, `workspace-census`, `knowledge-graph` (Reference Modules), and `co-change` (a new candidate — explained in §6.6). Of these, `inspect` is already in the FIRST-10-MINUTES-DESIGN recommendation; the other five are *newly derived here*, not yet in any prior design document.

The **recommended minimal product surface**: eight modules plus one Core capability. Each module maps to one or two canonical questions, no more. If a module answers zero canonical questions, it doesn't ship.

## 1. The full set of questions a senior engineer asks

Below is the *unmerged* list — every plausible question, grouped naturally. Consolidation happens in §2. Each question is written in the engineer's voice — first person, present tense, terse.

### 1.1 The "I just opened this" group — *Identify / Orient*

The first ten seconds. Pure disorientation reduction.

- I1. **What is this?** — a single sentence, then three sentences.
- I2. **What does it do?** — what is the user-visible behavior / output?
- I3. **Who owns this?** — who do I blame if it's broken? Who do I thank if it's good?
- I4. **Where do I start?** — what's the entry-point file, command, doc?
- I5. **What should I read first?** — what is the minimal read-set to be productive?
- I6. **Is this still alive?** — is the project maintained, or abandoned?
- I7. **Is this my project, or someone else's?**

### 1.2 The "show me what's there" group — *Map*

The first minute. Structure memorization.

- M1. **What is the architecture?** — the folders, the components, the boundaries.
- M2. **What are the dependencies?** — system, library, knowledge.
- M3. **What is canonical for X?** — for any concept, where is the single home?
- M4. **What is the test layout?** — where do I run tests, where are the fixtures, where is the CI config?
- M5. **What is the deployment shape?** — where does this run, how does it get there?
- M6. **What is the file inventory?** — how many files, what kinds, where are the docs.
- M7. **What is the conceptual graph?** — how do the files refer to each other?

### 1.3 The "show me what changed" group — *Audit*

The next question once orientation is solid. Drift and motion.

- A1. **What changed recently?** — what's new in the last week, month, quarter?
- A2. **What is stale?** — what's here that nothing references?
- A3. **What is missing?** — what would the workspace look like if it were "complete" by some criterion?
- A4. **What is risky?** — what would break the build, leak secrets, violate an invariant?
- A5. **What violates a principle?** — what isn't consistent with the rules this workspace holds itself to?
- A6. **What's untested?** — what's here without test coverage?
- A7. **What would I break if I changed X?** — impact analysis, pre-emptive.
- A8. **What is the work-in-progress?** — what's a WIP, what's a draft, what's done?

### 1.4 The "show me what to read" group — *Agent-readiness*

The 2026-shaped question. What does the AI need?

- R1. **What would an AI see reading this cold?** — what would it conclude, what's missing for it?
- R2. **What is the engineer's stable context here?** — what facts about the project does the engineer keep re-telling the agent?
- R3. **What files should an AI never read?** — secrets, build noise, dependency trees.
- R4. **What is the bootstrap order for an AI?** — which four files first, which four next.
- R5. **What would confuse an AI here?** — naming collisions, schema drift, conflicting docs.

### 1.5 The "tell me why" group — *Contextualize*

The deep question. Why is this the way it is?

- C1. **What decisions shaped this project?** — the *why* behind the *what*.
- C2. **Who decided each thing?** — what's the trail of decision-makers?
- C3. **What was rejected?** — what alternatives were considered and not chosen?
- C4. **What is the project owner's story?** — what is this person working on, what do they care about, what patterns do they reject?
- C5. **What's the work I'm doing?** — what am I here to do today, this week, this quarter?

### 1.6 The "I want to grow this" group — *Extension*

The last and arguably most underestimated group. The engineer doesn't just consume; they extend.

- E1. **How do I add a new module?** — *OperatorOS-specific, included because the project has extension.*
- E2. **How do I validate my change?** — what's the test loop?
- E3. **What can I remove without breaking it?** — what's replaceable?
- E4. **How do I keep the workspace consistent over time?** — what's the discipline that prevents drift?

### 1.7 What was left out (and why)

Several plausible questions were considered and dropped:

- "Is the code good?" — out of scope; that's a code-review tool's job, not a workspace tool's.
- "Are tests passing?" — that's a test runner's job.
- "What's the latest version?" — `git tag --sort=-creatordate` answers this trivially.
- "What's the license?" — `LICENSE` file answers this trivially; Convention over Configuration.
- "What's the security posture?" — out of scope unless secrets drift is in scope; this is `vault/`'s concern.
- "How do I deploy this?" — anti-positioning: OperatorOS is not a deployment tool (per `POSITIONING-VALIDATION-2026-07-15.md` "Why not Ansible"). The question is excluded to keep the discipline honest.

### 2.2 The grouping that the eleven fold into

Six *kinds* of question:

- **Identify** (Q1) — what *is* this?
- **Orient** (Q2 + Q11) — where do I start, and how do I grow it?
- **Map** (Q3 + Q7) — what's the structure, and what does an agent see in it?
- **Audit** (Q4 + Q5 + Q6) — what changed, what is missing, what is risky?
- **Contextualize** (Q8 + Q9 + Q10) — why, who, what for?

A senior engineer reads these six kinds as: *identify, orient, map, audit, contextualize, extend*. Or shorter: *name, start, see, audit, explain, grow*. That short version is the conceptual layer the rest of this document builds on.

### 2.3 The test for whether a new question belongs in the canonical set

A new candidate question passes the canonical-set bar if and only if:

1. It's distinct from the existing eleven (not a rephrasing).
2. It's answerable by a workspace tool — not by a code-quality tool, a CI tool, a deployment tool, a test runner, or a package manager.
3. It's asked often enough that an engineer would notice if the answer were missing (≥ once per quarter for typical work).
4. It's general enough that the question applies to ≥ 80% of engineering workspaces, not just one niche.

A question failing any of those four is not in the canonical set. It may be in `MODULE-ECOSYSTEM-DESIGN-v0.8.0.md` as a candidate module's *purpose*, but not in this canonical taxonomy.

### 3.3 What the ranking teaches

Three observations from the rank table:

1. **Q1 and Q2 are tied at the top.** Both are asked every visit; both are critical for the engineer to start. These are the first-impression questions; OperatorOS already invests in them via `bootstrap.md` and `inspect`.
2. **Q3 and Q7 are tied at #4.** Both ask about the workspace *as it exists* — Q3 from the engineer's perspective, Q7 from the AI's perspective. The fact that they're tied is the strongest evidence for OperatorOS's *core promise*: "engineer and AI in agreement about a workspace." Agreement is precisely the alignment of these two questions' answers.
3. **Q6 ("What is risky?") scores lower than expected.** A senior engineer *would* ask this, but they ask it once per quarter, not weekly — at the moments of major changes (refactors, releases, secret rotations), not at every visit. This is why `drift-detector` (an Audit-of-risks module) is important but not "critical" — it lands the answer once per quarter, not on every visit.

### 4.2 The unfilled question — Q9

**Q9 ("Who am I in this workspace?") has no canonical OperatorOS capability.** This is the strongest single finding of this document.

What's missing today:

- `bootstrap.md` is read first by the AI; it does not contain the *engineer's* identity.
- `IDENTITY.md` (when `identity-md` is installed) contains onboarding-interview answers — that answers *Q10* (what work), not *Q9* (who).
- `operatoros.yaml` has a `name:` field (workspace name) and a `preset:` field (preset name), but no field for the engineer's identity.
- `.project-state/<slug>/` is the engineer's *mission* identity, not their *workspace* identity.

What Q9 demands:

A canonical, schema-validated, machine-checkable place where the engineer answers "who am I, what kind of work do I do, what patterns do I reject, what is my preferred collaboration model with an AI agent." This is *engineer-level identity*, not workspace-level identity.

Why this matters:

- An AI agent arriving in the workspace today can read `bootstrap.md` for project context, `IDENTITY.md` for onboarding interview answers — but it cannot read who the engineer *is*. The engineer has to tell it again, every session.
- A second engineer reading a colleague's workspace has no canonical way to learn who the owner is, what they care about, what their conventions are — except by reading scattered README files.
- The methodology explicitly addresses engineer identity in `methodology/05-onboarding-interview.md` — but the interview's answers are *workspace-bound* (answers go into `IDENTITY.md`), not engineer-bound. There's a place for the engineer to declare who they are *across* workspaces.

**Recommended action.** This capability is a future sprint's deliverable. It may be called `engineer-profile` (Core capability or Reference Module), `operator-profile`, or `me-md`. The point isn't the name; the point is that this question is currently unanswered and is the only critical-tier question with no OperatorOS capability. (See §5 — Recommendation — for the place in the roadmap.)

## 6. The reclassifications — what each is and isn't necessary

Five candidates are flagged as reclassify — meaning they should not be built as standalone modules, with reason. Each is mapped to an existing or proposed capability that answers the same question better.

### 6.1 `preset-applier` (Workspace) — reclassify to thin Core wrapper

**What it claims to do.** Record every `operatoros apply` invocation with a timestamp and module-diff. Provide a `history` command.

**Question it answers.** Q11 (extension) only tangentially — it's about *how the preset was applied*, not *how to extend*.

**Better answer.** The existing `operatoros apply` command already writes a structured output (it prints which modules were installed). The History command is provided by **git log** against `operatoros.yaml` (which is updated on each apply). A dedicated module is unnecessary; a future Core command `apply-history` could be added if the team has evidence users want it.

**Reclassification.** Don't ship `preset-applier` as a v0.8.0 module. Track the "apply-history" question in the Kanban; if a real user asks for it, promote to a Core command in v0.9.0.

### 6.2 `catalog-validator` (Quality) — reclassify to `drift-detector` finding

**What it claims to do.** Validate the catalog against its schema and against invariants (content-hash consistency, etc.). Repair on `--confirm`.

**Question it answers.** Q5 (missing) and Q6 (risky).

**Better answer.** `drift-detector` already produces a Q5/Q6 finding per principle violation. The catalog-specific invariants are *one* principle check, not a separate concern. Adding `catalog-validator` as a standalone creates the appearance of a "second quality system" — which would invite users to run one but not the other, fragmenting the audit story.

**Reclassification.** Fold `catalog-validator`'s invariant checks into `drift-detector`'s "Typed Substrate" finding. Don't ship the module. If a future user needs the catalog validator *as a CI gate*, expose it as `operatoros run drift-detector check --principle=typed-substrate` (a sub-flag of drift-detector, not a new module).

### 6.3 `schema-bridge` (Quality) — reclassify to `operatoros validate` + `drift-detector`

**What it claims to do.** Verify all four core schemas are present and version-pinned.

**Question it answers.** Q5 (missing).

**Better answer.** `operatoros validate schemas/workspace.schema.json` already validates a single schema. The bridge's job is *reachability* (are all four schemas findable?) and *version* (do they match the version pinned in the workspace?). Both are folded into `drift-detector` checks: "are schemas v0.7 present?" is a Q5 question, and `drift-detector` already audits that the catalog schema is conformant.

**Reclassification.** Same as `catalog-validator` — fold into `drift-detector`, don't ship the module.

### 6.4 `preset-applier` History, `preset-card`, `philosophy-quotes`, `principles-card` — reject

All four fail the canonical-question test. They answer questions the engineer doesn't frequently ask:
- "What happened at apply-time?" — answered by `git log`.
- "What does this preset contain?" — answered by `cat presets/personal/preset.yaml`.
- "What are the philosophy quotes?" — not a question asked in a workspace.
- "What does a principles card look like?" — same.

The maintenance cost of each is non-zero; the answer frequency is zero (or near-zero in a senior engineer's workflow). Reject all four.

### 6.5 `identity-md` — reclassify the question it answers

**What it does today.** Generates `IDENTITY.md` from the onboarding interview (`methodology/05-onboarding-interview.md`). Five questions.

**Question it answers today.** Q10 (What work am I doing here?).

**Question it does NOT answer.** Q9 (Who am I in this workspace?).

The reclassification here is *about the question*, not the module. `identity-md`'s purpose is Q10, which is correct. The gap is Q9, which has no canonical capability at all (see §4.2). The fix is a *new* module that explicitly answers Q9 — call it `engineer-profile`, `operator-profile`, or `me-md`. See §7 below.

### 6.6 `knowledge-graph` — defer but don't reject

**What it does.** Parses cross-references in `*.md`, `*.yaml`, `*.json` and emits a graph.

**Question it answers.** Q3 (architecture), Q4 (what changed) — by allowing the user to query "who references what" — and Q5 (what's missing — orphans).

**The marginal case.** Q3 and Q4 are both answered partially by `architecture-index` and by `git log`/`catalog-timeline` (deferred). `knowledge-graph` would *deepen* the answer but not *fundamentally* change it. For a workspace with < 50 files, `find + grep` is comparable.

**Verdict.** Defer to v0.10.0. Add only when ≥ 3 users report they want a graph view. The 500-line implementation effort is non-trivial; the marginal frequency is below the importance threshold today.

### 6.7 One module suggested by the canonical-question analysis — `co-change`

**The unrepresented facet of Q4.** Q4 ("What changed recently?") is answered today by `git log`. But `git log` answers at the *file*-level: which files changed, by whom, when. It does not answer the *cluster*-level question: "what tends to change *together*?"

**The new module.** `co-change` analyzes `git log` history and emits a "files that change together" report. If `bootstrap.md` always co-changes with `modules/*/module.yaml`, the report highlights that; if a doc file never co-changes with anything, that's the orphan signal.

**Question answered.** Q4 (specifically the workspace-structure facet).

**Importance.** Useful — important for engineers doing refactors; not critical for orientation.

**Effort.** Low — ~200 lines, mostly git plumbing + a co-occurrence matrix.

**Recommendation.** Include `co-change` as a *candidate* for the v0.10.0 reporting release, not v0.8.0. The cost is small, the marginal value is real, and it answers a question that none of the other 10 ships set answer.

## 8. Mapping back to the core promise

> *"OperatorOS keeps engineer and AI in agreement about a workspace."*

The eleven canonical questions split into *engineer-facing*, *AI-facing*, and *shared-facing*:

- **Engineer-facing** (asked by the engineer alone): Q3 (architecture seen from the inside), Q4 (change seen from git), Q8 (decisions), Q9 (engineer identity), Q10 (engineer's work), Q11 (extend).
- **AI-facing** (asked by the agent alone): R1–R5 in the merged taxonomy — all folded into Q7.
- **Shared-facing** (asked by both with the same answer): Q1 (identity), Q2 (entry point), Q5 (missing), Q6 (risky).

The shared-facing group is where OperatorOS's *core promise* lives. If the answers to Q1, Q2, Q5, Q6 are the same when read by the engineer and the AI, agreement holds.

Of the 9 shipping modules + 1 Core capability:

| Capability | Q-answered | Engineer-facing | AI-facing | Shared-facing |
|------------|-----------|------------------|-----------|---------------|
| `inspect` | Q1, Q7, Q5, Q11 | — | — | ✓ |
| `context-builder` | Q1, Q11 | ✓ | — | — |
| `workspace-census` | Q5 | ✓ | — | — |
| `architecture-index` | Q3 | ✓ | — | — |
| `module-cookbook` | Q11 | ✓ | — | — |
| `bootstrap-md` | Q2 | — | — | ✓ |
| `identity-md` | Q10 | ✓ | — | — |
| `drift-detector` | Q5, Q6 | — | — | ✓ |
| `bootstrap-tier-refresh` | Q2, Q7, Q11 | — | — | ✓ |
| **GAP — `engineer-profile`** | **Q9** | **✓** | — | — |

**Diagnosis.** The four shared-facing capabilities answer the core promise directly. The three engineer-only capabilities (Q3, Q10, Q11 facets) build the engineer's own confidence in the workspace. The AI-only question (Q7) is partially answered via `inspect` and `bootstrap-tier-refresh`. The Q9 gap is the one place where the engineer-facing surface is weakest.

The mapping also shows: **the core promise has good shared coverage**, but the engineer's *own* question (Q9) is unanswered. Adding a future `engineer-profile` module would *strengthen* the engineer-facing leg of the promise — making it equally important to the AI-facing leg.

## 10. Summary — the canonical questions in one paragraph

A senior engineer asks eleven canonical questions of every workspace, in six groups: Identify (Q1 — what is this), Orient (Q2 — where do I start, Q11 — how do I extend), Map (Q3 — architecture, Q7 — what would an AI see), Audit (Q4 — what changed, Q5 — what is missing, Q6 — what is risky), and Contextualize (Q8 — what decisions, Q9 — who am I, Q10 — what work). Q1 and Q2 are critical at every visit; Q3 and Q7 are critical for the core promise ("engineer and AI in agreement about a workspace") to be visible. Q9 — *who am I in this workspace?* — is the single question that no OperatorOS capability currently answers, and the strongest finding of this discovery. The recommended minimal product surface to answer 8 of 11 well, 2 adequately, and 1 (Q9) as-yet-unanswered is **9 modules plus 1 Core capability**: `inspect` (Core), `context-builder`, `workspace-census`, `architecture-index`, `module-cookbook`, `bootstrap-md`, `identity-md`, `drift-detector`, `bootstrap-tier-refresh`, plus a future `engineer-profile` for Q9. Three modules from the v0.8.0 design (catalog-validator, schema-bridge, preset-applier) fail the canonical-question test and are reclassified — they fold into `drift-detector`, into `operatoros validate`, and into a future Core `apply-history` command respectively. Three more (preset-card, philosophy-quotes, principles-card) are rejected for failing the canonical-set bar. One module (`knowledge-graph`) is deferred for marginal importance today. One new module (`co-change`) is proposed as a v0.10.0 candidate for the cluster-level facet of Q4. The canonical-question analysis confirms the v0.8.0 ships-set direction and identifies the one gap (`engineer-profile`) most likely to demand future-module investment.
